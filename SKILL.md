# Restaurant Fire Safety Compliance

A practical compliance guide for commercial kitchen owners and operators. The goal is simple: help restaurant owners avoid grease fires, pass fire inspections, satisfy insurance requirements, and protect staff and property — without drowning them in code language they don't have time to read.

Most restaurant fires start in the cooking area, and the leading cause is failure to clean grease-laden surfaces. The codes below exist because kitchen exhaust systems collect highly flammable grease residue that, once ignited, can spread fire through ducts directly onto the roof. Compliance is not paperwork — it is the actual mechanism that prevents your restaurant from burning down.

---

## Who this skill is for

Trigger for anyone running or working in a commercial kitchen: independent restaurant owners, multi-unit operators, hotel F&B managers, institutional kitchen supervisors (schools, care facilities, churches), food truck operators with fixed cooking installations, and anyone preparing for a fire marshal inspection or insurance audit.

When the user is in **Alberta or anywhere in Canada**, default to citing the **National Fire Code – 2023 Alberta Edition (NFC(AE))** and **NFPA 96** together. When elsewhere, cite **NFPA 96** as the universal benchmark and note that local jurisdictions adopt it (sometimes with amendments). If the user is outside North America, ask which jurisdiction applies before citing specific code articles.

## The four questions every operator must be able to answer

When a user describes their situation, work toward getting clear answers to these four questions. If any answer is "I don't know" or "no," that is the compliance gap to address first.

1. **What is your cooking volume and fuel type?** This determines cleaning frequency. Solid fuel (wood, charcoal, mesquite) is the most demanding; high-volume wok and charbroiling are next; sit-down restaurants are mid-range; seasonal/low-volume operations are the least demanding.
2. **When was the entire system last cleaned to bare metal — hood, filters, ducts, fan, and rooftop?** "Filters cleaned in-house" does not count. Code requires the full system, top to bottom.
3. **Do you have a dated cleaning certificate posted in the kitchen, and a log of all past cleanings?** NFPA 96 §11.6.13 explicitly requires this — it is the first thing an inspector asks for.
4. **When was your wet-chemical fire suppression system (the cylinder above the cookline) last inspected?** Code requires every six months by a licensed technician. The tag on the cylinder shows the date.

If the user can answer all four cleanly, they are likely compliant. If not, the rest of this skill walks through what to do.

## Cleaning frequency — the one table to memorize

This table is from NFPA 96 (current edition) and is adopted by reference under National Fire Code – 2023 Alberta Edition Article 2.6.1.9. Use it whenever a user asks "how often."

| Type of cooking operation | Minimum cleaning frequency |
|---|---|
| Solid fuel — wood, charcoal, mesquite | **Monthly** |
| High-volume — 24-hour operations, charbroiling, wok cooking | **Quarterly (every 3 months)** |
| Moderate-volume — typical sit-down restaurant | **Semi-annually (every 6 months)** |
| Low-volume — churches, day camps, seasonal, occasional cooking | **Annually** |

Two important nuances to communicate:

- **These are minimums.** If grease accumulates faster (heavy frying, deep South Asian/peanut-oil cooking, ventless hoods, undersized systems), inspections must be more frequent. The actual rule in NFPA 96 §11.6.2 is that surfaces must be cleaned **before** they become heavily contaminated — frequency is just the default trigger.
- **"Cleaned" means cleaned to bare metal.** NFPA 96 explicitly requires removing grease deposits from interior surfaces of the entire system: hood, grease filters, plenum, horizontal duct runs, vertical riser, fan housing and blades, and rooftop curb area. Wiping the visible exterior of the hood is not cleaning.

## What is actually required — the recordkeeping and documentation checklist

Most restaurants fail inspections not because the system is dirty but because they cannot prove it has been cleaned. The documentation requirements are simple and inexpensive once a routine is in place.

**Required after every cleaning** (per NFPA 96 §11.6.13):
- Dated certificate or sticker posted inside the kitchen, naming the servicing company
- Written record listing any areas not cleaned (e.g., inaccessible duct sections requiring future access)
- Before-and-after photographs documenting condition (industry best practice; some insurers now require it)

**Required to be kept on file for the fire marshal:**
- A log of every cleaning service for the past 24 months at minimum
- Wet-chemical fire suppression system inspection certificates (every 6 months)
- Hood/duct/fan access panel locations documented (NFPA 96 §4.1.8 — all interior surfaces must be accessible)
- Operator training records if any kitchen staff are designated for daily filter cleaning

**Insurance and lease implications:** Most commercial property and business interruption insurers now require proof of NFPA 96 cleaning frequency. Failure to maintain records can void coverage in the event of a grease fire claim. Many commercial leases also assign hood cleaning responsibility to the tenant — tell the user to check their lease.

## What fire inspectors check — and what they often miss

Use this section when a user is preparing for a fire marshal visit, or when they ask "what does the inspector look at?"

**What a thorough inspection checks:**
- Date on the most recent cleaning certificate (and whether frequency matches operation type)
- Date on the fire suppression cylinder tag
- Removable filters in place, undamaged, properly oriented
- Fusible links above the cookline (replace annually)
- Access panels present, labeled, and not painted shut
- Visual condition of hood interior, plenum, and accessible duct sections
- Clear exhaust path from hood to rooftop fan
- Cookline appliances under the protected zone (suppression nozzles aimed correctly)

**What inspectors most commonly miss — and what an operator should still get cleaned anyway:**
- Horizontal duct runs (grease condenses in cold zones, out of sight)
- Fan housing scroll and blades (often only the visible exterior is wiped)
- Rooftop fan curb area (grease pools here and is the most common ignition point for roof fires)
- Hood plenum above the filter rack (filters get washed; the plenum behind them often does not)
- "Cleaned to bare metal" verification — chemical wiping the exterior gives a polished look while the interior remains coated

When a user asks whether they passed inspection or whether they are "good to go," remind them that a passed inspection does not always mean a clean system. The cleaner the entire system, the lower the actual fire risk regardless of what the inspector saw.

## Annual compliance calendar — give this to the operator

When a user asks "how do I stay on top of this," recommend a simple recurring calendar tied to their cooking type. Adapt the cleaning rows to the table above.

| Frequency | Task |
|---|---|
| Daily | Wipe hood exterior; empty grease cup/tray; clean filters in dishwasher or soak |
| Weekly | Visual check of filters (replace if damaged); check grease drip pans on the fan |
| Monthly | Visual check above the filters into the plenum; document anything unusual |
| Per cooking-type schedule | Full professional cleaning to bare metal — hood, ducts, fan, rooftop curb |
| Every 6 months | Wet-chemical fire suppression inspection by a licensed technician |
| Annually | Replace fusible links; review insurance compliance documentation; verify access panels intact |

## Red flags — when to stop and call a professional immediately

- Visible dripping grease anywhere — inside the hood, on the cookline, or on the rooftop near the fan
- Yellow or brown discoloration spreading on the rooftop around the exhaust fan curb
- Smoke "rolling out" of the hood instead of being drawn up cleanly
- A fire suppression tag dated more than 6 months ago, or no tag at all
- Any small fire that occurred in the cookline or hood, even if extinguished — the system must be inspected before reuse, and the suppression system likely needs recharging
- Insurance, fire marshal, or a buyer/landlord requesting cleaning records the operator does not have
- A new restaurant opening with a previously-used kitchen — never assume the prior tenant cleaned the system

## Common operator misconceptions to correct

- *"We clean our filters in the dishwasher every night, so we are good."* Filter cleaning is required but is a tiny part of compliance. The hood, ducts, and fan are not cleaned by washing filters.
- *"The hood looks clean from the outside."* Compliance is measured by interior bare-metal condition, not exterior shine.
- *"Our cleaner came last month — we're set for the year."* Cleaning is per cooking type. A wok kitchen cleaned in January is not compliant by April under NFPA 96.
- *"We don't cook much, so the rules don't apply to us."* The code applies to any operation producing grease-laden vapours, including low-volume operations — they just have annual minimums.
- *"The previous owner had it cleaned."* Always verify with documentation. Many takeover-fires happen in the first 90 days because the new operator inherited a clogged system.
- *"The fire marshal didn't say anything was wrong."* A passed inspection is not an absolution. The operator is responsible for the system regardless of what an inspector sees on a 15-minute visit.

## Code references

- **NFPA 96** — Standard for Ventilation Control and Fire Protection of Commercial Cooking Operations. Key sections: §11.6.2 (clean to bare metal), §11.6.13 (post a dated certificate), §4.1.8 (interior surfaces must be accessible).
- **NFPA 17A** — Standard for Wet Chemical Extinguishing Systems. Six-month inspection minimum.
- **National Fire Code – 2023 Alberta Edition (NFC(AE))** — Article 2.6.1.9. Mandatory in Alberta since May 1, 2024.
- **Alberta STANDATA 19-FCI-002R1 / 19-BCI-017R1** — Fixed Fire Suppression and Exhaust Systems – Cooking, Cleaning, and Maintenance.
- **Alberta STANDATA 19-FCI-008 / 19-BCI-027 / 15-PCB-002** — Service Provider Qualifications. Recognized programs: Phil Ackland's Kitchen Hood Cleaning School, IKECA, and MFS Trade School (Alberta-certified).
